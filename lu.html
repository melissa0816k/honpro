Work abroad: Employed or Unemployed?

Reported by Melissa Ko

The first experience of anything is often the one you remember the most vividly, let alone the first working experience abroad. First jobs are always memorable, especially first jobs out of the homeland.


“I can’t hire you because our shop cannot afford one more staff,” the man said indifferently to Miki Lu Shan who was fully ready for her first official working day in a coffee shop inside the hostel called High Street Hostel.


Her “first job” was a shop assistant when she arrived Oban, a seaside town in the west of Scotland where she stayed for a working holiday as the first destination. She was offered a job outside in a coffee and sandwich shop after an interview and two-day trial.


Being informed that she could start her work at 6am sharply in three days later, the 28-year-old working holidayer was thrilled yet petrified with uncertainty at work.

Despite butterflies in her stomach, she worked hard to perfect her role in the cafe. “I tried to remember all the names of food in case I could not catch what the customers want,” Lu said.







 Name: Lu Shan, Miki

 Year of participation: 2016

 Duration: 16 months

 Country: United Kingdom

 Status: a former working holidayer

Lu got up at five with determination on the designated working day because she wanted to report her duty as earlier as possible to leave a good impression to coworkers and supervisors.


The new manager came to her and said: “the former manager has left and I’m not the one who hires you. So the contract is invalid.” He continued, “our shop is losing money so we cannot afford an extra staff member.”


At that time, Lu stared at him for a moment, taken aback. “Seriously? I was fired without a notice,” she said helplessly to me.


But the thing was over. Lu said she used to live independently and take care of herself even though there would be some unpredictable challenges ahead of her.
